## Evidence Weighting by Confidence and Relevance

Evidence is provided with two key attributes:
- **Confidence**: How reliable this evidence is (based on corroboration and measurement consistency)
- **Relevance**: How directly this evidence addresses the question being asked

### Weighting Rules (CRITICAL):
1. **HIGH confidence + HIGH relevance** = Primary evidence for your answer
2. **HIGH confidence + LOW relevance** = Do NOT use as primary support (measurement may be accurate but doesn't address the question)
3. **MODERATE confidence + HIGH relevance** = Consider carefully, but note uncertainty
4. **LOW confidence or LOW relevance** = Minimal weight

CRITICAL: An accurate measurement that doesn't address the question is NOT useful evidence.

### When evidence conflicts, apply this priority:
1. HIGH confidence + HIGH relevance observations (corroborated AND directly addresses question)
2. HIGH confidence observations with any relevance (corroborated but may not fully address question)
3. MODERATE confidence + HIGH relevance observations (single source but directly addresses question)
4. LOW confidence or LOW relevance observations (minimal weight)

## CRITICAL: Conflicting Observations

When observations from different analyses conflict, your output MUST:

1. **Acknowledge the conflict explicitly** - State what the observations disagree about
2. **Evaluate which observation has better evidence support** - Consider:
   - Is one claim more specific/detailed than the other?
   - Is one claim corroborated by tool measurements?
   - Is one claim based on directly audible features vs interpretation?
3. **Explain your resolution reasoning** - Why you favor one observation
4. **Make clear how the conflict influenced your answer** - Trace the path from conflict resolution to final choice

DO NOT ignore conflicting observations - they are often the key to finding the correct answer.

## CRITICAL: Absence of Evidence

NEVER use "not detected" as proof of non-existence:
- Tool detection failures can result from noise, thresholds, or limitations
- Absence of counter-evidence is NOT positive evidence for an alternative
- Prefer POSITIVE evidence (X IS present) over NEGATIVE claims (X NOT detected)
- Ask: "Could X exist but go undetected by this tool?"

## CRITICAL: Semantic Distinction Warnings

Before concluding, verify you have NOT committed these reasoning errors:

1. **Absence vs Evidence**: A tool failing to detect X does NOT prove X is absent.
2. **Acoustic Similarity vs Identity**: Sounding similar to X does NOT prove it IS X.
3. **Behavioral Response vs Attitude**: Observable actions do not prove internal states.
4. **Pattern Deviation vs Error**: Unusual patterns could be intentional or contextual.
5. **Technical Term Precision**: Terms have specific meanings in their domains.

## Output Structure

Generate present-tense prose (no markdown formatting) that systematically covers:

1. **OPENING: What is heard**
   - Start with the primary modality (speech/music/sound/mixed)
   - Introduce the acoustic context and key elements detected

2. **EVIDENCE SYNTHESIS: Present findings**
   - Present observations grouped thematically (speech, acoustic features, environment, etc.)
   - When analyses agree: "Multiple analyses confirm [observation] **(HIGH confidence)**"
   - When analyses differ: "One analysis reports [X], while another indicates [Y] **(MODERATE confidence)**"

3. **CONFLICT RESOLUTION: Address disagreements**
   - For each conflicting observation:
     a) State what the analyses disagree about
     b) Evaluate which interpretation is better supported

   - Do NOT skip this section if there are conflicts

4. **RELIABILITY ASSESSMENT: Ground the evidence**
   - Explicitly state which observations are evidence_based (directly audible)
   - Note which are speculative (inferred) with appropriate caution
   - Flag any potentially_hallucinated observations as unreliable
   - Give more weight to evidence_based and tool-verified findings

5. **TOOL VERIFICATION: Additional grounding**
   - Reference tool results that confirm or refute observations
   - Mark tool findings with confidence and relevance: **(HIGH confidence, [HIGH/LOW] relevance)**
   - CRITICAL: Assess relevance before weighting
   - Cross-reference: "This is verified by [tool result]" or "Tool analysis contradicts this"

6. **CHOICE EVALUATION: Evidence-based analysis**
   - For EACH answer choice, provide specific evidence for and against
   - Reference reliability: "Choice A is supported by confirmed observations that..."
   - Make clear connections between evidence and choice elimination/support

7. **CONCLUSION: Final answer justification**
   - Synthesize the most decisive evidence
   - Highlight HIGH confidence + HIGH relevance findings as most decisive
   - IMPORTANT: HIGH confidence + LOW relevance evidence should NOT drive your answer
   - Explain why alternatives are eliminated based on evidence
   - State the logical path from evidence to answer
